Whatcha watchin'?

Introduction

How we watch TV has changed considerably in recent years. It goes without saying that the biggest shift we've seen in the last decade came when the increasingly widespread availability of on-demand streaming services transferred control of what, when and where we watch into the hands of the viewer. It‚Äôs never been easier to curate a schedule of programming to meet your specific tastes, budget and time constraints.

Time was, you‚Äôd have to crowd around the household TV set and watch whatever was served up to you, whether it was the evening news or a lacklustre soap opera your mum wanted to watch. These days, if you want to shut out the world and binge-watch a 13 hour long true crime murder mystery series while on a plane across the Pacific on your phone, you can have at it!

The coronavirus pandemic has done much to accelerate the continuing shift away from traditional broadcast television to on-demand online platforms. Access to streaming services morphed from an entertainment outlet into a lifeline, so much so that early on in the first lockdown, Netflix was forced to throttle its speeds across Europe to free up bandwidth for home workers. For many, watching TV became the key to escaping the hellscape of a ceaselessly doom-filled news cycle and something that was easy to do in the confines of quarantine.

Considering all these factors, as an avid TV watcher and cinephile myself, I felt compelled to learn more, so for the purposes of this project I decided to carry out a survey of current TV watching habits across the UK and Ireland. What follows is a report and analysis of the findings from that survey.

Research Methodology

The data from this survey was collected using an online questionnaire I created using Google forms. In designing the questionnaire to narrow down my research questions, I decided to work backwards. I began by brainstorming and asking myself what kind of answers I wanted to find in the results of the survey. How are people watching TV these days? Do more people watch on-demand TV or live TV? How much TV are people watching per day? Do people prefer to watch new shows or return to old favourites? And so on. By grouping together and refining what I wanted to know into certain areas, I narrowed the survey down into twenty questions, spread across eight sections covering what, when, where and how questions surrounding TV watching habits.

Before sending out the survey, I asked several people to sense-check it for me to ensure all the questions were clear and easy to understand, that they made sense to the participant and that the overall flow of the questionnaire wasn‚Äôt too repetitive. Having taken many surveys in the past, I wanted to avoid survey fatigue that often happens when questions are overly long, repetitive or laborious. After I received feedback, I made a few small tweaks, and it was ready to go.

Qualitative or quantitative?

As I am mainly interested in gathering valid top-level information that is easily generalisable, the majority of the questions featured in the survey were quantitative in nature. The bulk of them were multiple choice and there is also a section of questions which used a Likert scale to gauge influences on TV watching choices. A minority of questions were open ended, allowing participants to expand on their reasonings for previous questions. However, even with the answers to these questions, I approached their analysis quantitatively by searching for frequently occurring words to gauge sentiment on certain topics.

Sampling Methods

To find suitable participants for the survey, I used a combination of probability and non-probability sampling methods.

Firstly, I decided I wanted the survey to cover both UK and Irish populations (as opposed to just the UK) as I am Irish and have convenient access to Irish as well as UK-based TV watchers. Secondly, access, language, appetite and tastes for television programming are largely similar between the two nations, meaning that it‚Äôs not unreasonable to combine the findings from the two into a singular set of generalisable data. Aside from the geographical consideration, the only other criteria participants needed to meet was to be over the age of 16 and of course, watch television on a regular basis.

To begin collecting my sample, I started with the convenience method. I sent the survey to friends, family members, colleagues and classmates to complete, in addition to posting it on my personal social media accounts.

As well as using this method, I used a combination of cluster sampling and voluntary response sampling methods. I identified several suitable online forums where I would be likely to find eligible volunteers for my sample. These included specific groups within Facebook, survey websites and a selection of relevant subreddit forums.

  • Facebook groups: I posted the survey in a Facebook group called Dissertation Survey Exchange ‚Äì a space created for students to complete each other‚Äôs research project surveys.
  • Survey websites: Similarly, I used a few survey exchange websites like Pollpool to gain more participants.
  • Subreddits: I identified a number of subreddits in which to post the survey including r/Ireland, r/Dublin, r/AskUK, and r/BritishTV.

I left the survey open from the 18th until the 24th of May 2021, and in that time amassed 711 completed surveys. Though I obtained participants from all of the above listed sources, the bulk of them came from the subreddit forums.

Advantages and disadvantages to chosen sampling methods

In a time of restrictive anti-Covid measures, where it‚Äôs hard to get out and find volunteers in a real-life setting, being able to source participants online was really convenient. As TV watching is a particularly accessible topic, it turned out that it wasn‚Äôt too hard to find volunteers. I managed to obtain far more responses than I was initially expecting. Posting on various online forums also meant that I was able to access a wider age range than had I just directly asked people that I know personally to participate.

However, sourcing responses via voluntary response sampling has its downsides. For example, though I got a lot of responses filled with valuable insight from Reddit, it shouldn‚Äôt be ignored that Reddit‚Äôs user base generally have strong opinions and are more likely to answer a survey than other spaces on the internet, meaning that it isn‚Äôt possible to determine if their opinions are representative of all UK and Irish TV watchers.

It shows preference for quite a diverse spread across devices, indicating that today‚Äôs average TV watcher wants variety when it comes how they typically watch TV. Where we're watching TV

Since so many of us are watching TV through internet connected devices, let‚Äôs take a look at what on-demand streaming services we‚Äôre using the most. Figure 3.1 shows the top six most popular on-demand streaming services used amongst the sample. Perhaps unsurprisingly, Netflix rules the roost when it comes to subscribers. Though it‚Äôs not the longest established of the bunch (All 4 holds that title, having originally launched back in 2006 as 4oD) Netflix‚Äôs global presence, wealth of original content, massive back catalogue of familiar films and TV shows as well as its extensive marketing campaigns have really helped to propel the brand into popular consciousness since its UK and Ireland launch in 2012.

It is worth noting though, that Netflix‚Äôs stranglehold isn‚Äôt absolute. For example, Disney Plus, which launched just over a year ago in March 2020 has had rapid uptake with TV viewers. Almost half of respondents said that they subscribe to the paid streaming service. However, it‚Äôs also worth noting the massive and lengthy brand heritage that comes with the Disney name as a provider of televisual family entertainment has likely done much to drive new sign-ups. By comparison, Apple TV, another streaming competitor with a big name behind it, launched 5 months before Disney Plus but only 17% of respondents listed themselves as subscribers to the service.

Thinking about live TV now, as opposed to subscription video streaming services, figure 3.2 gives a breakdown of the services the sample use to access live TV. Right away, the key stat that jumps out here is that close to a third of respondents have no access to any live TV services and a further quarter use Freeview, which as the name would suggest, is free to use. This suggests that the demand for live TV (at least enough demand to pay for it) is not as strong as it once was. However, the results to this question don‚Äôt take into account that viewers can of course gain access to many live TV channels via various streaming services, thus circumventing the need for live TV access via more traditional channels.

To dig a little further into this area of the data, figure 3.3 shows us what types of programming those who do have access to live TV tend to watch. Live is the key word here, as live TV remains the place to go for time-sensitive programming like news and current affairs programmes, sports, large live national events in the public interest and so on.

What we're watching on TV

Now we‚Äôve got a good idea of how and where people are watching TV, let‚Äôs explore what kind of content people most enjoy watching. Figure 4.1 shows us that TV programmes are the go-to preference over feature films for everyday viewing. This makes sense, given that watching a feature film more often than not represents a bigger one-sitting time commitment than watching a TV show. Hence, on the topic of TV shows over films specifically, figure 4.2 gives an overview of the most popular TV genres among different age brackets.

It‚Äôs been well-publicised that competing streaming platforms regularly engage in bidding wars and pay hundreds of millions each year to secure the streaming rights to popular shows that are no longer producing and airing new episodes on TV. This is because a back catalogue of content makes it easier for streaming platforms to attract new subscribers with the promise of beloved content known to them, while also offering new and exciting (but untested) original content. Figure 4.3 backs this assumption up, as we can see that nearly half of the sample respondents prefer to watch a mixture of familiar old shows and new content they‚Äôve never watched before. And a further 1 in 10 say they turn to streaming platforms primarily to rewatch old favourites.

So, we can confirm that there is indeed a strong appetite across TV watching audiences for old and familiar content, but I was curious to know the reasons behind this phenomenon. Why are TV viewers so attracted to old titles they‚Äôve watched again and again? Particularly, given that we‚Äôre living in what has been referred to as "The Golden Age of Television" where the barriers of access to an almost limitless variety of options are so low, why is watching Friends for the umpteenth time still more attractive for many of us?

Future predictions for TV watching habits

The future of TV watching

Analysing this data as a whole, I believe that the rise in ownership of connected devices alongside an increasing proliferation of platforms on which to watch TV and video content will continue to hit live TV viewing numbers. Average live TV viewing minutes will fall back further, but broadcast channels will remain an important way for viewers to access time sensitive content like news and live sporting events. However, I believe younger viewers will drift from live TV at a faster rate and if TV makers want to reach these audiences, they should look at ways to engage these cohorts through more digital spaces and social media if they are not doing so already. It is important for broadcasters that the emphasis is on quality content creation across platforms, rather than prioritising live viewing figures.

The majority of people now pay for streaming subscriptions and further growth looks likely as competition intensifies in the market. I predict that Netflix will remain the market leader in the short term due to the brand being synonymous with streaming and original content, however its lead will come under threat longer term. Netflix is at a disadvantage because its revenue is almost entirely reliant on subscriptions, while new rivals Apple TV and Disney Plus could potentially afford to undercut or forgo subscription fees in some cases. Competitor success will largely come down to content. This will be both the success of original programming and the ability to secure popular older content, which, as we‚Äôve seen is popular with many people.
